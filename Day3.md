1. Normal Prompt (No Role Assigned)
   
Here's a practical guide to preparing for an IT job interview:
Understand the Role

Read the job description carefully and identify the key technical skills required
Research the company's tech stack, products, and recent news
Tailor your preparation to the specific role (developer, sysadmin, network engineer, etc.)

Technical Preparation
Brush up on core concepts:

Data structures & algorithms (especially for dev roles — LeetCode, HackerRank)
Networking fundamentals (OSI model, TCP/IP, DNS, HTTP/S)
Operating systems (Linux commands, processes, memory)
Databases (SQL queries, normalization, indexing)
Security basics (common vulnerabilities, firewalls, encryption)

For coding roles specifically:

Practice 2–3 problems daily on LeetCode (start Easy → Medium)
Know time/space complexity (Big O notation)
Be comfortable coding on a whiteboard or shared editor

For system/network/cloud roles:

Review certifications relevant to the role (CompTIA, AWS, Cisco, etc.)
Be ready to troubleshoot scenario-based questions

Practical Tips

Set up your environment — make sure your IDE, compiler, or tools work before a remote interview

Know your resume cold — be ready to explain every project and technology listed
Mock interviews — practice with a friend or use Pramp / Interviewing.io
Review past projects — be able to walk through architecture, decisions, and challenges

Day Before

Get a good night's sleep
Review your notes lightly — don't cram
Test your audio/video if it's a virtual interview
Have your resume and a notepad ready

2. Developer Persona

Claude responded: Software Developer Interview Prep GuideSoftware Developer Interview Prep Guide
1. Master Data Structures & Algorithms (DSA)
The most tested area in dev interviews. Focus on:

Arrays & Strings — sliding window, two pointers
Linked Lists — traversal, reversal, cycle detection
Trees & Graphs — BFS, DFS, binary search trees
Dynamic Programming — memoization, tabulation
Sorting & Searching — quicksort, mergesort, binary search
Hash Maps & Sets — frequency counting, lookups

Practice platforms: LeetCode, HackerRank, CodeSignal, NeetCode.io

💡 Aim for 2–3 problems/day. Start with Easy, move to Medium. Most companies test Medium-level.


2. Core CS Fundamentals
TopicWhat to KnowOOPInheritance, Polymorphism, Encapsulation, AbstractionOSThreads, processes, memory management, deadlocksNetworkingHTTP/S, REST, TCP/IP, DNSDatabasesSQL joins, indexing, ACID, normalizationSystem DesignScalability, caching, load balancing, microservices

3. Know Your Tech Stack Deeply
Be ready to answer deep-dive questions on your primary language and frameworks:

Language internals (e.g., how Python handles memory, JavaScript event loop)
Framework-specific questions (React lifecycle, Spring Boot annotations, etc.)
Version control — Git commands, branching strategies, merge vs rebase
Testing — unit tests, mocking, TDD basics


4. System Design (for Mid/Senior Roles)
Practice designing real systems:

Design a URL shortener (like bit.ly)
Design a chat application
Design a ride-sharing backend

Key concepts: Load balancers, CDN, databases (SQL vs NoSQL), caching (Redis), message queues (Kafka)
📚 Resource: "Designing Data-Intensive Applications" by Martin Kleppmann

5. Behavioral Questions (Don't Skip This!)
Use the STAR Method (Situation, Task, Action, Result):
Prepare stories for:

A bug that was hard to fix
A disagreement with a teammate/manager
A project you're most proud of
A time you missed a deadline
A time you learned something quickly


6. Coding Interview Strategy
When given a problem:

Clarify — Ask about edge cases, input size, constraints
Brute force first — State a naive solution
Optimize — Improve time/space complexity
Code — Write clean, readable code
Test — Walk through with examples and edge cases


Never jump into coding silently — interviewers want to see your thinking process.


7. Resume & Portfolio

Keep resume to 1 page, tailored to the job
Quantify impact: "Reduced API response time by 40%" not "Improved performance"
Have 2–3 solid projects on GitHub with clean READMEs
Be ready to do a live code walkthrough of your projects


8. 4-Week Prep Plan

9. Tools & Resources

Pramp / Interviewing.io — free mock interviews
Grokking the System Design Interview — system design

10. Final Checklist Before the Interview

 Research the company's tech stack and products
 Prepare 5+ STAR stories
 Test your coding environment (for remote interviews)
 Prepare smart questions to ask the interviewer
 Review your resume line by line
 Good sleep the night before!

3. Founder Persona

Claude responded: Preparing for an IT Job Interview — Founder's PerspectivePreparing for an IT Job Interview — Founder's Perspective
As a founder interviewing for an IT role, you have a unique profile — strong leadership and vision, but you may need to reframe your experience to fit an employee mindset. Here's how to nail it:

1. Reframe Your Founder Experience
Interviewers may wonder: "Can they work in a team? Follow processes? Take direction?"
Address this proactively:

Translate startup wins into employee-friendly language
Focus on execution, not just vision
Emphasize collaboration over solo decisions

Founder LanguageInterview Language"I built the company""I led a cross-functional team of 5 engineers""I wore many hats""I have hands-on experience across full-stack, DevOps, and product""We pivoted""I adapted quickly to changing requirements and constraints""I raised funding""I managed stakeholder relationships and delivered under pressure"

2. Be Honest About Why You're Making the Switch
Interviewers will ask this. Have a clear, confident answer:

Avoid sounding desperate or like the startup failed badly
Frame it positively — learning, stability, impact at scale, collaboration
Example: "I built and scaled a product from scratch. Now I want to apply those skills in a larger engineering environment where I can go deeper technically and collaborate with world-class teams."


3. Highlight Your Technical Breadth as a Strength
As a founder, you likely touched many areas. Use this:

Full-stack development experience
Infrastructure and DevOps (if applicable)
API design, database architecture, scalability decisions
Security and compliance decisions
Vendor/tool evaluation experience


Position yourself as a high-ownership engineer who thinks beyond just writing code.


4. Prepare Technically (Don't Skip This)
Many founders underestimate this. Companies still expect technical rigor:
For coding interviews:

Refresh DSA — do 30–50 LeetCode problems (Easy + Medium)
Practice on a whiteboard or shared editor (not your own IDE)
Revisit language fundamentals if you've been more managerial lately

For system design (your strong suit as a founder):

You've likely made real architectural decisions — talk through them
Design problems you've actually solved (auth systems, payment flows, scaling)
Know the trade-offs: SQL vs NoSQL, monolith vs microservices, REST vs GraphQL


5. Show You Can Be a Team Player
This is the #1 concern hiring managers have about founders:

Share examples of listening to engineers and changing your mind
Talk about mentoring or unblocking teammates
Demonstrate you can work within constraints (budget, timeline, tech debt)
Show humility — "I made this decision and it was wrong. Here's what I learned."


6. Leverage Your Unique Founder Superpowers
Things most candidates can't offer that you can:

Product thinking — you understand user needs, not just tickets
Business context — you write code with ROI in mind
Speed and ownership — you ship, iterate, and take responsibility
Cross-functional communication — you've talked to customers, investors, designers
Hiring and team-building experience — valuable for senior/lead roles


Frame these as multipliers on your engineering value.


7. Target the Right Roles
As a founder, some roles fit better than others:

8. Prepare Smart Questions to Ask
As a founder, your questions should reflect strategic thinking:
